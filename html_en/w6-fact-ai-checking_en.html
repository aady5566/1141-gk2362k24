Psychology: The Brain's Cognitive Shortcuts

Confirmation Bias

The tendency to search for, interpret, and reinforce information that aligns with our existing beliefs.

(The pursuit of being right!)

Motivated Reasoning

We use extremely harsh standards to examine views we disagree with, striving to find their flaws or errors in order to refute them.

(Insistence on you being wrong!)

Cognitive biases enable false information to spread widely and be difficult to correct

When we find content that supports our own views, we get a psychological pleasure from dopamine that drives us to continue engaging with it.

When information that challenges or questions our core beliefs appears (such as a fact-checking report), we don't look at it objectively. Instead, we activate psychological defense mechanisms. We feel extremely uncomfortable with views we disagree with, so we use extremely harsh standards to examine them, striving to find their flaws or errors in order to refute them.

Fake News Spreads Faster and Wider

A 2018 study from MIT published in "Science" analyzed the propagation paths of about 126,000 news stories on Twitter. The study found that false news was 70% more likely to be retweeted than true news. True news took about six times longer than false news to reach 1,500 people. The reason behind this is that fake news is often more "novel" and more likely to inspire strong emotions like fear and disgust.

Emotion is the Key Driver

A research team at New York University found that on social media, posts with "moral-emotional" words (e.g., shame, evil, cruel) increased the share rate by about 20% for every 10,000 followers. This demonstrates that both algorithms and human psychology favor content that draws lines between "us" and "them" and incites group emotions.

Source:Emotion shapes the diffusion of moralized content in social networks

The Situation in Taiwan

According to the Reuters Institute's 2023 Digital News Report, the overall trust in news among the Taiwanese public is only 29%. Even more noteworthy is that as many as 43% of Taiwanese people say they find it difficult to distinguish between real and fake news online. This shows that our information environment is full of challenges, and the improvement of personal media literacy is urgently needed.
