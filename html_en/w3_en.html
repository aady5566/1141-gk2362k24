Main Types of Machine Learning

Supervised Learning

Data Type: Uses "labeled" data for training, meaning each input has a corresponding correct output or answer.

Goal: To learn the mapping function between input and output data to predict the output for new, unseen data.

Example Application: Predicting flight times (output) based on past weather conditions (input).

Unsupervised Learning

Data Type: Uses unlabeled, raw data for training.

Goal: To explore the data and discover hidden patterns, structures, or relationships, rather than predicting a specific output.

Example Application: Finding similarities in customer groups (clustering) or identifying new associations in unlabeled data.

So, What is Deep Learning?

Deep Learning

Relationship with Machine Learning: It is a subset of machine learning, belonging to a specific class of machine learning models.

Characteristics: Uses multi-layered neural networks (similar to the human brain's structure) to learn complex patterns in data.

Example Application: Deep learning models can perform supervised learning tasks, like image recognition, as well as unsupervised learning to extract valuable information from large amounts of unlabeled data.

The Core of Machine Learning:

Supervised Learning & Unsupervised Learning

The choice of technical path reflects whether we want to "validate known answers" or "explore unknown structures" in the data.

Key Questions

How do we balance "guidance" versus "exploration" when training an AI with a "test paper with standard answers" (Supervised Learning) to solve known problems, compared to exploring the unknown and correlations in a "vast library without answers" (Unsupervised Learning)? And what are the risks?

Discussion in Different Scenarios

1. The World of Personalized Music:

How does Spotify recommend music to you? One way is supervised: you "like" songs (providing clear labels), and the system finds and recommends songs with similar musical features. Another way is unsupervised: the system analyzes the listening data of hundreds of millions of users worldwide, automatically "clustering" people with similar tastes, and then recommends songs that your group likes but you haven't heard yet (e.g., "Discover Weekly"). Which method makes it easier to break out of your filter bubble and discover new music genres? And which method might pull you deeper in?

2. Coupang's E-commerce Marketing Strategy:

Coupang wants to understand its customers. A supervised approach is to conduct satisfaction surveys, having customers rate items like "price," "service," and "quality" on a scale of 1-5 (providing clear labels), and then analyzing which aspect has the lowest score. An unsupervised approach is to directly analyze hundreds of thousands of raw customer reviews, where the AI automatically clusters the comments and might discover an unexpected complaint theme, such as "excessive, non-eco-friendly packaging" or "poor quality of free gifts," which weren't options in the original survey. Which method can solve known problems? Which can discover unknown ones?

3. Combating Financial Fraud:

How do banks detect credit card fraud? The supervised method is to use past transaction records marked as "fraudulent" and "normal" to train a model. When a new transaction comes in, the model determines the probability of it being fraudulent. But if a completely new fraud technique appears, the model might not recognize it. The unsupervised "Anomaly Detection" method, on the other hand, first learns what a "normal" transaction pattern is (a large cluster). Any transaction that doesn't fit this pattern, regardless of the technique, is marked as suspicious. Which method is better at stopping known enemies? Which is better at dealing with unknown threats?

4. Insights into Urban Safety:

A police department wants to prevent crime. The supervised method is to analyze data marked with "crime location" and "time" to predict the next crime hotspot. But this relies entirely on "reported" data. The unsupervised method is to analyze anonymous communication or traffic flow data in the city to find areas of "abnormal gathering" or "sudden pattern changes." This could be an undiscovered black market or just a flash mob. How should we weigh the opportunities and risks of misinterpretation that come with this type of exploration?

Unseen Risks: From "Entrenched Bias" to "False Discoveries"

Supervised Risk - The Bias Problem

AI learns from biased historical data, not only replicating but also amplifying these biases. Principle: Garbage in, garbage out.

Disparity in AI Recognition Error Rates

ref: (MIT) Media Lab's "Gender Shades" study (Joy Buolamwini)

Unsupervised Risk - Difficulty of Interpretation and Spurious Correlations

The "patterns" found by unsupervised learning do not necessarily have real-world significance. It might discover that ice cream sales and drowning incidents are highly correlated. (Correlation does not equal causation).

There's Also "Reinforcement Learning"

(Reinforcement Learning, RL)

It doesn't require label comparison, nor does it simply discover patterns from data spontaneously like unsupervised learning.

It learns strategies to achieve goals progressively through interaction with an environment, based on rewards or punishments for its actions. (Much like humans)
